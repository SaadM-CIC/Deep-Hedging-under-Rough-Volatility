# Deep Hedging under Rough Volatility

An empirical comparison between classical hedging (delta hedging) and Deep Hedging, across three market models of increasing complexity: Black-Scholes, Heston, rBergomi. The project aims to demonstrate numerically — not merely assert — under which conditions Deep Hedging outperforms analytic hedging, and under which conditions it does not.

## Repo structure

```
src/dhrv/          main package (models, hedging, evaluation, figures)
tests/             test suite, one validation criterion per research phase
reports/figures/   figures generated by src/dhrv/reports/make_figures.py
```

Reproduction: `poetry install`, then `poetry run pytest` for the test suite, `poetry run python -m dhrv.reports.make_figures` to regenerate all the figures cited below.

## 1. Three models, an increasingly uncomfortable market assumption

The project's throughline: Black-Scholes assumes constant volatility — an assumption contradicted by the implied volatility smile observed in the market. Heston fixes this by making volatility stochastic, but remains **Markovian**: the state `(S_t, v_t)` summarizes the entire system, which contradicts the long-memory behavior documented empirically by Gatheral, Jaisson and Rosenbaum (2018) in *Volatility is rough*. rBergomi (Bayer, Friz, Gatheral, 2016) repairs this last crack by injecting a Volterra process with a singular kernel — at the cost of losing any closed-form delta.

**`reports/figures/trajectories_comparison.pdf`** visually illustrates this increasing roughness: simulated paths under BS, Heston and rBergomi over the same horizons, with a perceptible irregularity that increases from one panel to the next.

**`reports/figures/implied_vol_smile.pdf`** confirms the expected quantitative signature: at short maturity (T=0.1), Black-Scholes remains by construction a flat line (no smile possible with constant `σ`), Heston produces a moderate skew that flattens quickly away from the money, and rBergomi shows the characteristic short-term ATM skew explosion — exactly the power-law behavior `τ^(H-1/2)` documented by Bayer-Friz-Gatheral, absent from Heston by Markovian construction. We also numerically validated (Phase 5.3) that the empirical rBergomi skew exponent is consistent with `H - 0.5` within a reasonable statistical tolerance, in line with theory.

## 2. Classical delta hedging: where the Markovian assumption cracks

### Black-Scholes — the reference
BS delta hedging reproduces the known theoretical convergence (Bertsimas-Kogan-Lo 2000): the variance of the discrete hedging P&L decreases as O(1/n) with rebalancing frequency. Numerically validated, Monte Carlo price consistent with the closed-form formula within standard error.

### Heston — an interesting empirical nuance
A result worth mentioning explicitly rather than sweeping under the rug: the "naive" Heston delta (`P1`, taken directly from the Heston (1993) characteristic function) **does not systematically beat** the naive BS delta under strong leverage (`ρ = -0.7`). The reason, documented by Alexander & Nogueira (2007), is that `P1` measures the option's sensitivity to `S` while holding `v` fixed — whereas with strong `ρ`, `S` and `v` co-vary, and this cross-exposure is not captured by `P1` alone. The **minimum-variance delta** (`Δ_MV = P1 + (ρ·ξ/S)·Vega`) corrects this bias and does beat the naive BS delta, as expected. It's a good reminder that "the theoretically correct delta" and "the delta that actually minimizes hedging variance" don't always coincide once leverage is present — a subtlety a project that simply computed `P1` and stopped there would have missed.

### rBergomi — the definitive break
With no closed-form delta available, a proxy is used: BS delta with instantaneous volatility `√v_t`, explicitly documented as an approximation rather than presented as an exact delta. The performance degradation is clear and measured: the variance of the hedging P&L under rBergomi (proxy) is about 4x higher than the one obtained under Heston with the correct MV delta, on the same protocol. This is the central scientific result of this part of the project — not a sign of a broken implementation, but the numerical demonstration that non-Markovianity genuinely degrades the hedging quality achievable by classical analytic methods.

## 3. Deep Hedging: what Buehler et al. (2019) predict, and what we observe

The formulation follows Buehler, Gonon, Teichmann and Wood (2019): instead of computing a Greek, a neural network is trained to directly minimize a convex risk measure (entropic risk measure) of the terminal P&L, end-to-end and differentiably.

### Mandatory sanity check
Before training on more complex models, we verified that a deep hedger trained under Black-Scholes with no transaction costs does recover the analytic BS delta (mean absolute error < 0.14 over the test grid) — the roadmap flags this test as blocking, and rightly so: a first attempt revealed a genuine autograd graph connectivity bug (a missing P&L update line), invisible without this safeguard.

### `reports/figures/pnl_distributions.pdf` — frictionless comparison
Across the three underlying models, with no transaction costs, **Deep Hedging does not beat any of the analytic baselines**: P&L standard deviation roughly 40% (BS), 50% (Heston), 40% (rBergomi) higher than their respective classical counterparts. This is not an unexpected underperformance: Buehler et al. explicitly anticipate it — in a frictionless market, a well-specified analytic replication remains hard to beat; Deep Hedging's advantage shows up elsewhere (frictions, non-Markovianity poorly handled analytically).

### `reports/figures/transaction_cost_gap.pdf` — the founding paper's real thesis
This is precisely where Buehler et al.'s paper predicts a reversal: in the presence of proportional transaction costs, Deep Hedging should progressively overtake classical delta hedging, the latter only adapted to frictions through ad hoc corrections (Leland 1985). What we observe: the variance gap in favor of the classical approach **shrinks** as κ increases (from -0.52 at κ=0 down to a smaller residual gap at κ=0.005 after extending training to 1500 epochs), without however turning positive on the compute budget used here. A targeted diagnostic (measuring the average turnover of the learned policy) confirms the network does learn the right qualitative behavior — turnover reduced by 25% between κ=0 and κ=0.005 — pointing to a training-budget convergence issue rather than a conceptual flaw in the approach. This is documented here as a compute-resource limitation, not as a refutation of the theoretical result.

### `reports/figures/robustness_degradation.pdf` — model misspecification
When evaluating (without retraining) both the Heston deep hedger and the classical MV delta on paths generated with a `ρ` progressively different from the one used at training/calibration, both methods degrade — but Deep Hedging degrades **more slowly** than the analytic delta (factor ×1.15 vs ×1.22 at the largest tested deviation). A plausible explanation: the network, trained on simulated paths rather than on a fixed formula, may have captured a policy less rigidly dependent on the correlation parameter than the closed-form MV delta is. A result to be taken with caution (a single perturbed parameter, a single model tested) but consistent with the robustness argument sometimes made in favor of data-driven approaches.

### `reports/figures/policy_heatmap.pdf` — where the two strategies diverge
The policy learned by the BS deep hedger follows a shape similar to the analytic delta in the central region of the grid (S close to S0, where training actually exposed the network to data), but departs sharply at the extremes (S far from S0). This is not a local learning error: it is an out-of-distribution extrapolation effect, consistent with the data-driven nature of Deep Hedging — its reliability remains bounded by the coverage of the training sample, unlike an analytic formula valid over the entire domain.

## 4. Honest limitations of the project

- **Deep Hedging FFN vs LSTM under rBergomi**: contrary to the theoretical intuition (long memory → should favor a recurrent architecture), the LSTM tested performs slightly worse than the FFN on the protocol used (std 2.54 vs 2.22). The most likely cause is relative under-training of the LSTM (more parameters to tune) rather than a genuine absence of benefit from recurrence — unresolved in this version of the project, flagged as an improvement avenue.
- **Transaction costs**: Deep Hedging learns the right qualitative behavior (reduced turnover) but does not yet quantitatively beat classical delta hedging in variance on the compute budget used — a residual gap documented rather than hidden.
- **Robustness**: only one axis of misspecification tested (correlation `ρ` under Heston) — other parameters (H, η under rBergomi) remain an open extension.
- **Home-advantage bias**: Deep Hedging is trained to minimize a risk measure (entropic risk), but evaluated here on slightly different metrics (mean, standard deviation, VaR, CVaR) — a deliberately neutral evaluation, not favorable to the network by construction.
